PART 1

Why Intellectual Preparation

FROM THE TDOE SUMMER SESSIONS

The Intellectual Preparation Cycle

1
Study
Read the lesson as written. What is the target skill? Where will students do the thinking?
2
Annotate
Write access points into the guide — for the students in front of you, by name and need.
3
Rehearse
Practice the model, the think-aloud, and the directions — out loud, before the lesson.
4
Reflect
After teaching: did the access point work? Student work tells us — and feeds the next annotation.
What we annotate in planning is what walkthroughs look for and what student work measures — one coherent system.
PART 2

The Annotation Protocol

ANNOTATION PROTOCOL • PARTICIPANT GUIDE P. 2

Four Access-Point Moves

Visual Aids
What will students see? Anchor charts, icons on directions, gesture cues, sound–spelling cards.
Student Discourse
When will every student talk? Turn & Talk, choral response, sentence starters, partner rehearsal.
Model & Think-Aloud
What thinking will I make visible? Scripted think-aloud, worked example, modeled writing.
Activate Prior Knowledge
What do students already know? Yesterday's sound, a familiar text, a home-language connection.
WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE

One Annotation, Worked Example

"Access Point — Student Discourse & Visual Aids: I know my students struggled with /ee/ yesterday, so I am going to make an anchor chart with /ee/ and have students compare it to /ae/ with a Turn & Talk."
— a first-grade teacher's notes, written directly in the teacher guide
Why this annotation works
Names the moves — discourse + visual aids, not "differentiate."
Cites evidence — yesterday's struggle with /ee/, not a hunch.
Plans the action — the chart to make, the comparison students will talk through.
