PART 1

What Makes a Support a Scaffold

THREE TESTS • PARTICIPANT GUIDE P. 2

Temporary. Specific. Grade-Level.

Temporary
It has a planned exit. When the student no longer needs it, it comes down — that's the goal, not the failure.
Student-specific
Chosen for named students from evidence — a count, a work sample — not sprayed across the whole class.
Grade-level intact
The task stays the same. The scaffold raises the student to it — it never lowers the task to the student.
Missing Tier I access points show up later as Tier II referrals. Scaffolds planned now are interventions we won't need later.
PART 2

The Toolkit

FOUR TOOLS, MATCHED TO EVIDENCE • PARTICIPANT GUIDE P. 2

Each Tool Answers a Need You Can See

Anchor charts
When work shows: confusing similar sounds or spellings — /ee/ vs /ae/, b vs d. Build it with students; point to it in the moment.
Turn & Talk structures
When counts show: the same few voices every time. Assigned partners + "say it with your partner first" gets every student rehearsing.
Sentence starters
When work shows: one-word answers or incomplete sentences — especially for ELs. Posted, practiced, and faded over time.
Modeled writing
When work shows: students stall at the blank page. Think aloud while writing; leave the model up as students try their own.
